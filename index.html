Key takeaway

Bridging finance is short-term, asset-backed capital that closes a timing gap between two transactions or between a purchase and a longer-term facility. It is commercial and business-purpose, arranged through non-bank lenders, and priced to reflect the security, the loan-to-value ratio and the strength of the exit. Terms typically run from one to twelve months. It is not consumer credit and is not available for owner-occupied home purchase.

Bridging Finance Australia Explained

A bridging loan solves a specific problem: you need capital now, but the source of your long-term funding or the proceeds from a sale are not yet available. The gap may last weeks or several months. The bridging facility covers that window and is repaid once the gap closes.

Common scenarios in the commercial and non-bank context include settling a property purchase before an existing asset has sold, releasing equity tied up in a completed project while refinancing is arranged, and holding a site or asset through a short approval or pre-sales period before construction funding takes over.

How a Bridging Loan Is Structured

A bridging facility is interest-only for its term. The principal is repaid in full at the end when the exit event occurs, whether that is a settlement, a refinance or the drawdown of a longer-term facility. Monthly interest is either paid as it falls due or, in some structures, capitalised into the loan balance and settled at the end.

The loan-to-value ratio applied to the security determines how much can be borrowed. The lender will assess the exit strategy with care: the more certain and near-term the exit, the stronger the position the borrower is in when negotiating terms. A vague or extended exit timeline increases risk for the lender and that is reflected in pricing and the maximum LVR offered.

Security is typically a registered first or second mortgage over the property. In very short-term situations where speed is the priority, some lenders will accept a caveat over the title, though this provides a less robust security position and terms will reflect that accordingly.

Bridging Finance Versus Other Short-Term Structures

Bridging finance is one of several short-term structures available in the non-bank market. Understanding the distinctions helps borrowers identify the right tool for a given situation. A caveat loan is shorter in term and faster to settle, secured by lodging a caveat on the property title rather than a registered mortgage, making it appropriate for urgent settlement gaps where time does not permit a mortgage to be registered.

A residual stock loan is used after practical completion of a development, where unsold dwellings remain on the developer's balance sheet. It refinances the construction facility and releases the equity locked up in finished stock while the sales campaign continues. This is distinct from a bridging loan, which typically operates before or during a transaction rather than after completion.

A second mortgage ranks behind an existing first mortgage over the same security and can be used to unlock additional equity from a property without disturbing the first mortgage, but the second-ranking position carries higher risk and pricing reflects that. Costs and What Drives Pricing

Bridging finance costs more than standard bank debt. That premium exists because of the short term, the speed of execution, the deal-by-deal credit assessment and the risk profile of the situations where bridging is used. The relevant comparison is not the rate in isolation but whether the cost of the facility is justified by the outcome it enables.

The main pricing drivers are the LVR against the security, the quality and timing of the exit strategy, the ranking of the security (first versus second mortgage), the term of the facility and the nature of the asset. A low-geared deal with a clean and near-term exit prices very differently to a stretched, second-ranking position with a less certain exit.

Fees commonly include an establishment fee charged at the outset and, in some cases, a line fee or exit fee. The total cost of the facility needs to be assessed against the full term, not just the headline monthly rate, particularly where interest is being capitalised rather than paid monthly. The Exit Strategy: Why It Matters

The exit strategy is the most important element of a bridging loan application. Lenders will assess it in detail because the loan is designed to be repaid from a specific event. A credible, near-term exit reduces lender risk and strengthens the borrower's negotiating position on rate and LVR.

Common exits include the unconditional sale of an existing property, the drawdown of a longer-term refinance facility, the settlement of a presales program sufficient to support a construction loan, or the completion of a leasing or approval process that unlocks the next stage of funding. The less defined the exit, the more conservative the lender will be on terms.

Borrowers who approach a bridging facility with a clear exit timeline, documented in writing where possible, are better placed than those who present a scenario where the exit depends on a series of events each of which carries its own uncertainty.

Regulatory and Licensing Context

Bridging finance arranged for commercial and business purposes in Australia sits outside the consumer credit regime. It is not subject to the National Consumer Credit Protection Act 2009, which governs owner-occupied home loans and personal borrowing. Commercial borrowers dealing with non-bank lenders for business-purpose bridging facilities are not covered by the same disclosure and conduct rules that apply in the consumer space.

The Australian Securities and Investments Commission regulates financial services and credit more broadly. Borrowers seeking to understand the regulatory framework that applies to their situation should consult ASIC's published resources or seek independent legal advice. The Mortgage and Finance Association of Australia represents brokers and intermediaries who arrange non-bank lending, and its published standards give an indication of the conduct expectations that apply to professionals in this market.

Common questions

Can bridging finance be used for residential property purchases in Australia? In the non-bank commercial context covered here, bridging finance is a business-purpose product. It is not available for owner-occupied home purchases or personal borrowing, which are governed by consumer credit legislation. Residential developers and investors purchasing for investment or development purposes can use commercial bridging facilities, provided the loan is structured as business-purpose credit.

What happens if the exit does not occur within the agreed term? If the exit event is delayed, borrowers typically need to seek an extension from the lender. Extensions are not automatic and are subject to the lender's assessment at that point. Costs will generally increase for an extended term. In situations where the exit fails to materialise, the lender retains the right to enforce their security position. This is why the quality and realism of the exit strategy is assessed so carefully at the outset.

Is bridging finance available for commercial and industrial properties? Yes. Non-bank bridging facilities can be arranged against a wide range of asset classes, including commercial, industrial and specialised properties, provided the security and the deal story stack up. The lender will assess the asset, the LVR and the exit in the same way as a residential security, but will also consider the liquidity and saleability of the specific asset type.
